The Class That Breathes

Chapter 1 The Last Class

No one ever imagines the end of the world smells like floor polish and chalk dust.

That morning, the classroom hummed with its usual inertia: the low scrape of chairs, the scratch of pens on notebooks, the half-hearted sighs of students who had already been awake too long. The sun slanted lazily across the city, brushing over concrete and glass with a golden hue that seemed reluctant to illuminate a world already tired. The sky looked tired, almost reluctant to be blue—the kind of day that warned you without words, if you were paying attention.

It was the second period. Computer Science. Mr. Dev’s class—the man who made syntax sound like philosophy and sarcasm sound like oxygen. He stood at the front, sleeves rolled up, holding a whiteboard marker like a weapon. “Today,” he said, “we will learn recursion. The art of making the same mistake again and again, until your program—or your life—crashes.”

Half the class groaned. I smiled. He wasn’t wrong.

Outside, the sky began to hum—that low, static vibration that had become part of city life ever since the airborne alerts started flashing on the news. We all knew something was spreading, something invisible. But the government said it was “contained.” They always say that before they disappear from the broadcast.

In the classroom next to ours, someone coughed.

It sounded wet.

Then came another.

Then several, in a rhythm that no longer felt human.

Siddharth turned his head, frowning. “They’re still doing PE, right?”

Mr. Dev opened his mouth to answer, but the coughing turned into retching. A chair scraped violently against the floor. Something fell. Something heavy. And then silence. The kind that doesn’t fade.

Mr. Dev lowered his marker. The sound of its cap clicking back on was the loudest thing in the room.

I don’t know what moved me. Instinct, maybe. I crossed the room and slammed our windows shut. The metal frames groaned like they didn’t want to obey. Then I locked them.

All of them. I drew the curtains tight.

Everyone stared at me like I’d lost it.

Mr. Dev raised an eyebrow. “Planning a surprise party?”

I almost laughed. “Just… something felt wrong.”

It did. The air outside wasn’t moving right. The trees in the courtyard weren’t swaying—they were holding still, like they were listening too.

Minutes dragged. We waited for someone to check in from the next class. No one did.

When Tanya—the class bully—got up to peek through the curtain, I stopped her. “Don’t.”

She rolled her eyes. “You think air’s going to bite me?”

She didn’t open it. Not then.

Mr. Dev, with that eerie calm of his, walked to his desk, sat down, and sighed. “Well,” he said, “attendance is finally improving.”

We laughed. Not because it was funny, but because laughter felt like proof we were still alive.

By noon, the laughter died. The corridor smelled faintly like copper and rot. Something was leaking—not from the ceiling, but the air vents.

Aarav said his head hurt. Ishani went quiet. Arjun adjusted his glasses every five seconds, like he was trying to focus on something that wasn’t real.

That’s when the alarms started. Not the fire kind. The evacuation ones.

The school’s loudspeakers crackled, stuttered, then said just one word before dying again: “CONTAINMENT.”

Then nothing. No teachers. No footsteps. No screams. Just the soft hiss of dying electricity.

We waited. We hoped someone would come.

They didn’t.

By evening, the light through the curtains turned gold, then gray, then the color of paper ash.

I don’t know who said it first, but someone whispered, “We’re running out of air.”

We had closed everything. Sealed ourselves perfectly.

We had saved our lives… and trapped them too. The paradox hung in the room, taut and suffocating.

Mr. Dev looked at us one by one—seven students, one teacher, eight souls suspended between panic and denial. He spoke softly, like someone giving a bedtime story to fear itself.

“Alright, troops. We’ll need supplies. Food, water, air. Any volunteers?”

Siddharth raised his hand before I could stop him. “I’ll go.”

I did too. “You’ll need someone to carry the stuff.”

Arjun hesitated—the smallest, youngest of us—but then stood up. “I’ll come. For oxygen.”

Mr. Dev nodded slowly. “Smart. Team three. Bring me back something worth breathing for.”

We tore strips of fabric for makeshift masks, soaked them in bleach from the lab. The hallways felt endless now, the kind of silence that vibrates through bone.

We split near the main stairs.

I went to the staff room. Everything smelled stale—milk turned sour, coffee abandoned mid-sip. I found a half-box of chips, some fruit, and what looked like salvation in a steel tiffin.

Sid raided the vending machines for water. He came back, panting, shirt wrapped around his face like armor. “Got enough,” he gasped. “Barely made it past the library, the doors there are stuck.”

I smiled under my mask. “You always make it.”

We waited for Arjun.
Ten minutes.
Twenty.
An hour.

When he stumbled back, the light in his eyes was gone. “Guys, I- I think I touched something… something bad”. He was drenched in sweat, shaking, muttering half-words. Then came the vomiting—loud, violent, relentless.

Everyone froze. Tanya backed into the corner, hand over her mouth. “He’s infected.”

Mr. Dev said nothing. His jaw tightened. His hand twitched toward him—then stopped.

Arjun’s breathing slowed. He looked at me—just for a second—terrified. Then he tried to say something. His mouth opened. Nothing came out but blood.

And before any of us could move, Tanya acted. She didn't shove him toward the sealed window, but she cornered him, toward the reinforced steel door to the hallway. The moment he stumbled across the threshold, she slammed the door shut. The deadbolt mechanism engaged with a final, chilling thunk.

Tanya leaned against the door, panting, her eyes wide with a manic purpose. "He's infected," she hissed. "We don't share air with the dead."

Silence. The faint, horrifying sound of Arjun retching and desperately beating against the metal faded until all we heard was his final, strangled gasping, sealed out in the toxic air.

The Aftermath

We all stared at the door, the finality of the lock painting our faces in orange grief.
Aarav whimpered. Ishani turned away.
Mr. Dev closed his eyes and whispered, “Well… that escalated quickly.”
The words hung like smoke. No one laughed this time.
The air inside felt heavy. The air outside felt deadlier. I couldn’t tell which prison was worse.

We barricaded the door with chairs and desks. Every breath felt measured. Every movement sounded like it would summon catastrophe.

Hours passed. Shadows stretched across the ceiling, painted in the dim orange glow of the sunset. Tanya muttered curses under her breath. Sid hummed nervously, an attempt at calm, while Aarav huddled silently, tears streaming down his face.

I checked the makeshift masks again. The bleach had dried, leaving a thin, bitter film across our faces. It reminded me that survival had a taste—one that lingered unpleasantly on the tongue.

Mr. Dev moved among us slowly, inspecting our barricades, our masks, our bodies. He paused beside me, eyes scanning the room. “Discipline,” he said quietly, “is breathing in rhythm with fear. Control the small things and the large ones might ignore you.”

I nodded. He turned away, adjusting a chair against the door. Even his calm was unsettling—the kind that tells you the world is worse than you imagine.

Night deepened. The hum outside became sharper, pressing against the walls like the city itself was holding its breath. We whispered plans: rotating shifts, rationing, assigning lookout points. Every suggestion carried the weight of death.

By midnight, hunger was a faint shadow behind panic. By one a.m., the fear of suffocating pressed on our chests as heavily as the fear of infection. Every cough, every drip of liquid somewhere in the building, made us flinch. Somewhere far away, a bell rang. Just once.

Hollow. Faint. An echo of normalcy that was no longer real.
No one moved. We didn’t breathe too loudly. The air itself seemed hostile.

I caught Sid’s eye. He gave a small, trembling nod. It was agreement—not hope, but acknowledgment that we were still alive. That in itself was enough. I huddled with the others, trying to remember what normal felt like. The smell of chalk dust. Floor polish. The static hum. The laughter. The sunlight. All of it was still there, trapped beneath a thin, cruel layer of apocalypse.

And I realized—this was only the beginning. The school building became our cage, our sanctuary, our tomb.
We were six students and one teacher. Alive, yes. But how long before the air itself betrayed us?
We waited.
And we breathed. Carefully.

The problem wasn't what was outside. It was what was sealed in here with us—Tanya, the fear, and the rapidly shrinking air. I had to find a way to stop the rhythm, or we wouldn't last the night.
