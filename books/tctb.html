A Novel by Laasya

The Class That Breathes

Seven souls. One room. A sealed equation.

ACT ONE
The Last Class

Recursion, Mr. Dev is saying, is the art of making the same mistake again and again until your program or your life crashes.

Half the class groans. I smile.

He is not wrong.

He stands at the front of the room with his sleeves rolled up and a whiteboard marker held loosely in one hand, like a conductor who has already decided the orchestra is beyond saving. The board behind him says: f(n) = n × f(n-1). Below it, in smaller writing he has added as an afterthought: also applicable to bad decisions, unhealthy attachment to syntax, and coming to school on Fridays.

Second period. Computer Science. The room smells the way it always does, a particular combination of whiteboard ink, the industrial floor polish the janitors use every Tuesday, and the faint stale warmth of monitors that have been running since seven in the morning. It is the smell of ordinary. I have never thought to catalogue it before. I am cataloguing it now.

That should have been my first warning. I catalogue things when something feels wrong.

Someone has taped a paper heart to the corner of the board. Red and slightly crooked. Valentine's Day. Mr. Dev has left it there. He is not the kind of man who removes things that still mean something to someone.

I know everyone in the room the way you know people you have spent three years sitting adjacent to, not deeply, but precisely. Details accumulate even when you are not trying.

Siddharth is in the back row. He always takes the back row, not because he wants distance but because he takes up so much space that the back row is the only place he does not feel guilty about existing. He is built like someone designed for a different, more physical century, and he moves through the world with the permanent, slightly apologetic energy of a person who keeps accidentally knocking things over. Right now he is tapping his pen against his notebook in a rhythm that has nothing to do with anything Mr. Dev is saying. Stress-tapping. He does it during exams too.

Aarav sits three seats to my left and slightly in front, where I can see him in peripheral vision without turning my head. He is small and wears wire-rimmed glasses that he adjusts approximately every four minutes whether they need adjusting or not. He is adjusting them now. He adjusts them when he is thinking hard, when he is bored, and when he is afraid, which means the gesture tells me very little about his internal state on any given occasion.

Tanya sits near the window. She always sits near the window, not for the light, but for the exit. She has the particular stillness of someone who is always, on some level, calculating whether staying is worth it. She is watching Mr. Dev with an expression that is not quite boredom and not quite contempt. Something in between. Something colder.

Ishani is two rows ahead of me. She is the only person in the room who appears to be genuinely listening, not taking notes, just listening, her chin resting on one hand, her eyes following Mr. Dev the way you follow someone you trust to take you somewhere interesting.

Arjun is beside her. Arjun is always beside her, not because of anything romantic, but because proximity to Ishani is calming and Arjun needs calm the way the rest of us need oxygen. He is the youngest in the class by almost a full year. He is gentle in the particular way that very precise people are gentle, careful with things, careful with words, careful with the feelings of others. He is drawing something in the margin of his notebook. I cannot see what.

And Mr. Dev. Forty-something, permanently rumpled, fluent in three programming languages and one dialect of sarcasm so specific it has no name. He has been teaching at this school for eleven years. He makes syntax sound like philosophy and deadlines sound like suggestions. He is the only teacher I have ever had who seems to genuinely enjoy the people in the room with him, even when, especially when, they are failing spectacularly.

He is mid-sentence when the coughing starts.

It comes from the classroom next door.

One cough. The kind that interrupts itself, that breaks in the middle and comes back wrong. The kind that sounds like something trying to work and discovering it no longer can.

Then a second. Then several, overlapping, losing their rhythm.

Mr. Dev does not stop speaking immediately. He finishes his sentence, something about base cases, about the importance of knowing when to stop calling yourself recursively, and then he pauses, marker still raised, and tilts his head slightly the way a person does when they are deciding whether a sound is their problem.

Siddharth has turned in his seat. "They're still doing PE, right?" he says. "That's coming from the gym corridor."

Nobody answers him.

The coughing changes. It stops sounding like coughing and becomes something harder to name, a heaving, rhythmic sound that has evacuated the category of human discomfort and entered somewhere else entirely. There is a scrape. A chair. Something that lands heavily on the floor and does not get back up.

And then nothing. The specific nothing of a sound that has finished permanently.

Mr. Dev lowers his marker. The click of the cap going back on is the loudest thing in the room.

I do not know what moves me. I have thought about it and I still do not have an answer that satisfies me. It is not reasoning. Whatever moves me is older than reasoning. I am across the room and closing the windows before I have consciously decided to do it.

The metal frames groan. I lock them. All of them. I pull the curtains shut.

Everyone stares at me.

Mr. Dev raises one eyebrow. "Planning a surprise party?"

"Something felt wrong," I say.

Outside, the trees in the courtyard are completely still. I notice this through the gap at the edge of the curtain. There is no wind. The trees are not moving at all. They are the kind of still that is not peace. It is attention. Like something is listening.

That is when I see it for the first time. A flicker of white in the air vent above the chalkboard. Gone before I can fix my eyes on it. The size and speed of something small and alive, a streak of brightness in the dusty dark of the vent cover, there and then not there, like a thought that arrives and immediately loses confidence.

I note it the way I note things I cannot immediately explain: filed, pending, not yet useful.

Nobody else is looking at the vent. Everyone is looking at the window, or at Mr. Dev, or at the door to the corridor where the sound has come from and has now stopped.

We wait. That is the second period, the third, the long slow erosion of the morning into noon. We wait for someone to come check on us. Nobody comes. The corridor outside our door has the specific quality of silence that accumulates in empty spaces, and it is accumulating fast.

"The air outside is not moving right," Arjun says quietly. He is at the window, not opening it, just standing near it, his forehead almost touching the glass. "Look at the flag on the main building. It has completely dropped. Zero wind. That is not normal for this time of day."

He is right. I have already noticed. I have been triangulating wrongness since the moment I closed the windows, the stillness outside, the smell beginning to drift from somewhere, faint and metallic, like copper with something older underneath, and the fact that we have now been in this room for three hours without a single adult checking in.

Aarav adjusts his glasses. "My head hurts."

"Drink some water," Ishani says.

The smell is getting stronger. It is coming from the vents. The CO₂ is building. Seven people in a sealed room, the math is not comforting. I do not share it.

The loudspeaker crackles at 2:59 p.m. We all hear it, the click of a live mic, the hiss of a system that still has power. We turn toward it with the particular attention of people who have been waiting for a very long time.

One word. Staticky, compressed, cut short at both ends.

CONTAINMENT.

Then the hiss. Then the click of the mic going dead. Then nothing.

No teacher. No footsteps. No explanation.

Mr. Dev sits down on the edge of his desk. He sets the whiteboard marker down. He looks at us, seven students, one room, one word, and says nothing for a moment. When he speaks, it is quiet.

"Alright," he says. "Attendance is finally improving."

Nobody laughs. But something shifts, some of the rigid animal panic in the room loosens fractionally. Not because it is funny. Because it is human, and human is what we need to remember we still are.

We have closed ourselves in. Sealed ourselves well. By the time we understand the full shape of what we have done, the light through the curtains has turned from gold to gray to the colour of ash.

Saved our lives, I think. Trapped them too.

I do not say this out loud. I file it under things that are true and not yet useful.

"We will need supplies," Mr. Dev says. "Food, water. And we need to understand the air situation before it understands us. Volunteers?"

Siddharth raises his hand before I can stop him.

I raise mine anyway. "You will need someone to carry things."

Arjun stands up. He pushes his glasses up and looks at Mr. Dev with the careful steadiness of someone who has already considered the risk and decided to go anyway. "I will come," he says. "For the oxygen situation. I know where the chemistry stores are."

Mr. Dev looks at him for a moment. "Smart," he says. "Team three. Bring me back something worth breathing for."

The door to the corridor is in front of us. Behind it, the specific silence of a school that has become something else entirely.

We tear strips from spare cloth, an old lab coat hanging by the door, a scarf someone has left on a hook. We wet them at the classroom sink and tie them over our mouths and noses. It will not filter a chemical agent. It will do very little against an airborne pathogen. But it is a barrier, however thin, and barriers matter when you are trying to stay calm enough to think.

I open the door.

The corridor smells of copper and something sweet and wrong underneath, the smell of something biological that has stopped working. I do not know then that it is the last time things will be almost normal. The last time the classroom will be just a classroom, the last time the seven of us will all be in the same place without something irreversible having already happened.

I know it now. I know it now and I am writing it down, because someone should.

The Silence After Bell

The corridor does not answer us.

We stand in it, three of us, the door to the classroom sealed behind us with the specific finality of a decision you cannot take back. The hallway looks exactly as it should. The noticeboards are still up. Someone's forgotten water bottle is still sitting on the windowsill at the far end. The fluorescents are on.

Nothing is out of place.

That is the problem. Something has happened in this building and nothing is out of place, which means whatever has happened has been fast enough that there was no time for anything to fall, spill, or shift. Fast enough that the water bottle is still upright. Fast enough that the world has ended in between one moment and the next, neatly, without disturbing the noticeboards.

I file this and keep moving.

"Staff room first," I say. My voice comes out level. I am grateful for that.

The staff room is unlocked. This is the first small mercy of the day. It is also undisturbed in the same unsettling way the corridor is undisturbed. Someone has left a mug of tea on the table, half finished. A newspaper is open to the crossword, three clues filled in, one started and abandoned mid-letter. A cardigan hangs over the back of a chair. Ordinary things. The fossils of an ordinary morning.

I do not stop moving. I cross to the cabinets and start opening them methodically, top to bottom, left to right. The mind needs a system when it is trying not to think about what the stillness means.

Chips. A banana going soft. Two apples. A steel tiffin container, sealed, with the satisfying weight of something solid inside. A half-empty box of biscuits. I leave the tea and the coffee, caffeine increases respiratory rate, which increases CO₂ output, and CO₂ is already the variable I am most worried about.

Sid is at the vending machine in the corner. He breaks the glass and begins pulling out water bottles with focused, efficient force. He lines them up on the floor.

"How many?" he asks.

"As many as you can carry without slowing down."

He pulls out eight more. He can carry them all. I have known he can carry them all before I answer, which is why I answer the way I do.

Arjun has drifted toward the window. He is standing to one side of it, not directly in front, careful not to be visible from outside, looking at the courtyard below at an angle.

"There are people out there," he says.

Sid and I both go still.

"Not moving," Arjun says. His voice is very careful. "They are standing completely still."

I cross to him. Four people in the courtyard below. Standing at irregular intervals, not grouped together, not in any configuration that suggests they have gathered with any intention. One near the gate. One in the middle of the path. Two near the tree line.

Perfectly still. Not the stillness of people waiting. Not the stillness of people watching. Something else entirely. The stillness of something that is present but no longer inhabited, the lights left on after everyone has gone.

"We need to go," I say.

We leave the staff room. We move back down the corridor, faster now. We split near the main stairs. Sid will check the ground floor vending machines. Arjun will go to the chemistry storage for emergency oxygen canisters. I will return to the classroom and begin calculating exactly how much time we have.

"Twenty minutes," I say. "Back at the classroom in twenty minutes."

Sid says fine. Arjun says yes. He adjusts his glasses. He is afraid. He is going anyway. I note both things.

I think about the volume of our classroom. A person at rest consumes roughly 0.3 litres of oxygen per minute. Seven people. Siddharth's consumption will be higher. That is approximately 2.2 litres per minute for the group. I need to know the leak rate of the sealed windows, the rate at which CO₂ is accumulating.

CO₂ toxicity begins to affect cognitive function at around 1 percent. At 3 percent, headaches and difficulty concentrating become significant. At 5 percent, it becomes dangerous. We are already past the point where someone has a headache, which means we are already past the point where I should have started these calculations.

I walk faster.

Sid comes back in eleven minutes. He says the ground floor is empty. He says he has gone as far as the library corridor and the doors there are jammed shut, something heavy against them on the other side, and he has not tried to open them. I do not ask what he thinks is on the other side. He does not volunteer.

We wait for Arjun.

Ten minutes. Then twenty. The room is quiet. Mr. Dev sits on the edge of his desk and says nothing, which is the first time I have ever seen him choose silence voluntarily. Tanya is in the corner near the window, knees drawn up, watching the door with the expression of someone running their own calculations. Ishani is sitting next to Aarav. Her hand is not quite touching his arm, just close enough that he will know it is there.

At 4:34 p.m. we hear footsteps in the corridor.

Everyone hears them at the same moment. I know this because I watch seven people hold their breath in unison without any of us deciding to.

The footsteps are uneven. They come closer. They stop outside the door. The handle moves.

It is Arjun.

He comes through the door and he is wrong in the way that things are wrong before you can name the wrongness. Sweating through his shirt. Shaking in a way that is not cold and not fear alone, a tremor that starts somewhere internal and surfaces through his hands, his jaw, the faint involuntary movement of his head. He is still holding his notebook.

He says, "Guys, I think I touched something."

His voice is his voice. That is almost worse somehow.

"I think I touched something bad."

He looks at me when he says it. Not at Ishani. Not at Mr. Dev. At me. The look of someone handing a problem to the person they have decided is best equipped to solve it, even when the problem is themselves.

I look back at him and I run the variables and I do not let my face do anything that will cost him something in the next few seconds.

"Sit down," I say. "Right there. Breathe slowly."

He sits. He breathes. He tries.

The vomiting starts four minutes after he sits down. It is loud and violent and does not stop when it should have stopped. Aarav makes a sound in the back of his throat and moves toward the far wall. Ishani stands up and then freezes, caught between instinct and the fear of what instinct will cost her. Mr. Dev's hands go to the edge of his desk and grip it white.

Tanya backs into her corner.

Arjun looks up at me between one wave and the next. His face is the colour of old paper. He is trying to say something. I watch him try and I watch it fail and then the blood comes, and after the blood comes a silence that is total and absolute and nothing like any silence I have catalogued before.

It is the silence of a subtraction. A permanent one.

None of us move. For a moment we are simply people in a room with something that has no category yet.

Then Tanya moves.

She does not touch him. She uses the angle of her body and the direction of her movement and the fact that Arjun is no longer capable of organised resistance. She moves him toward the reinforced steel door to the corridor the way you move something you refuse to touch, with proximity and intention rather than contact.

The door closes. The deadbolt engages with a sound that is very small for the size of what it means.

Tanya leans against the door. She is breathing fast. Her eyes have the specific look of someone constructing, in real time, the version of an action they will be able to live with.

"He was infected," she says. Her voice is not quite steady. "You all saw it. We cannot share air with the dead."

Nobody speaks.

From the other side of the door, sounds. I am not going to catalogue them. Some things do not need to be data. Some things are simply what they are, which is unbearable, and then they stop, and the stopping is its own kind of unbearable, and you do not file either of them anywhere because there is no file for them. You carry them instead.

Mr. Dev closes his eyes. When he opens them he walks to Arjun's desk, the one that is now empty, and picks up the small wire-rimmed glasses that have been left there when Arjun grabbed his notebook for the supply run. He places them on the desk carefully, parallel to the edge, exact. It is the only thing he can do and he does it with everything he has.

He opens his mouth. The joke assembles itself, I can see it, the familiar reflex of a man who has used wit as armour for so long it deploys automatically. Then he looks at the glasses and it does not come. He says nothing.

The room is orange with late light. We are six now. The air tastes different from the air of an hour ago and I can no longer separate the chemistry from the weight of what we have watched and not stopped.

I sit down on the floor. I take out my phone. I open the calculator.

I have to start somewhere. The numbers are the only place I know how to start.

Outside, through the gap in the curtain, the courtyard is still. Somewhere below us, in the part of the building we have not yet gone to, something is making a sound that is not the building and not the wind and not anything I have a name for yet.

Thrum. Hiss. Thrum. Hiss.

Rhythmic. Regular. Mechanical. I note it. File it. Not yet useful. But close.

The Air Turns Heavy

Nobody sleeps.

That is the first thing I catalogue about the night, the way it refuses to become unconsciousness for any of us. We sit or lie in the particular stillness of people pretending to rest while their minds run at full capacity, burning through oxygen they cannot afford to waste on thinking, and thinking anyway because the alternative is feeling, and feeling is worse.

The classroom has reorganised itself in the dark. Not deliberately, not by any instruction, but the way people arrange themselves when they are frightened and trying not to show it. Siddharth has his back against the wall beneath the windows, knees up, head down. Ishani is near the door, close enough to hear anything in the corridor. Aarav has pulled two chairs together and is curled across them, his glasses folded on the floor beside him, his notebook open on his chest. Tanya has taken the far corner, the one with the most walls between her and everything else, sitting facing outward with the specific watchfulness of an animal that has claimed its territory.

Mr. Dev has not moved from his desk.

I am on the floor between the supply bag and the door, with my phone in my hand and the calculator open and the numbers arranged in front of me like a problem I can solve if I stare at it long enough. I cannot solve it.

The classroom is approximately 200 cubic metres. Six people at rest consume roughly 1.8 litres of oxygen per minute collectively, with Siddharth's consumption elevated, so closer to 2 litres per minute for our group. CO₂ at 0.5 percent causes mild symptoms. Aarav has complained of a headache hours ago. We are at or above 0.5 percent already. CO₂ at 1 percent causes significant cognitive impairment. At 3 percent, judgment becomes unreliable.

We have no way to measure it. I am calculating blind.

The oxygen canisters Arjun had gone to find are not coming. That variable is gone.

I put the phone down and press my fingers against my temples and breathe slowly and think about what we actually have. We have the room. We have the supplies. We have six people in varying states of functional capacity. We have the thrum and hiss sound coming from somewhere beneath the floor, which I have not yet been able to classify.

Aarav wakes up at some point before three in the morning, or maybe he has never been asleep. He sits up abruptly, sending his notebook sliding to the floor, and makes a sharp noise that is not quite a word.

Everyone in the room who has been pretending to sleep stops pretending.

"The sound," he says. He is looking at the floor. Specifically at the section of linoleum nearest the back wall, where the thrum and hiss has been strongest all night. "The sound is wrong."

"We know there is a sound," Tanya says from her corner. "It is probably the building. It is probably nothing."

"It is not nothing," Aarav says. "The period is wrong. Listen. The hiss is exactly half the duration of the thrum. That is not how mechanical systems fail. When systems fail they become irregular. This is regular. This is deliberate."

The room is quiet enough that we can all hear it now that he has named the pattern.

Thrum. Hiss. Thrum. Hiss.

He is right. It is precise. The interval between each cycle is identical. Whatever is making that sound is not malfunctioning. It is working exactly as intended.

Mr. Dev has lifted his head from his desk. "Explain the significance," he says.

Aarav scrambles off the chairs and onto the floor, pressing his palm flat against the linoleum. "I know this system. I have been in these walls. The old HVAC, the ventilation system that was decommissioned in 2018, it only ever ran one specific mode after shutdown. Emergency bio-isolation mode. And that mode was linked to the Level B Chemistry Lab filtration unit in the basement." He pauses, his hand still flat on the floor. "It is not pushing air in. The duty cycle is wrong for that. It is pulling air out."

I am already on my feet.

"It is removing air from this room," I say.

"From all the sealed areas on this floor," Aarav says. "Filtering it down. To the lab."

The calculation restructures itself instantly. We are not simply losing air to CO₂ accumulation. We are losing air to active extraction. The timeline I have been running is wrong, too generous by an unknown factor.

"We have to find it," I say. "We have to stop it."

"Or we find what it leads to," Aarav says quietly. He is still looking at the floor.

Tanya stands up from her corner. "Nobody is opening any floor. Nobody is going anywhere. We stay in the room. We stay sealed. That is the only thing keeping us alive."

"The room is what is killing us," I say.

Her jaw tightens. "You do not know that."

"I have the numbers. Would you like them?"

She would not like them. I know she would not like them. I say it anyway because sometimes the most useful thing a number can do is end an argument before it costs too much air.

We find the pump at 3:47 in the morning. It takes Siddharth to move the floor tiles, and it takes everything Siddharth has. He has been awake all night with the specific exhaustion of a large body running on insufficient oxygen. He does not complain. He takes the metal-plated dictionary from Mr. Dev's shelf, a book that has been sitting there so long it has become part of the furniture, and he levers it into the hairline gap between two tiles at the back corner of the room, directly above where the sound is strongest, and he pries.

The tile comes up with a sound of old adhesive releasing. Cold air rushes upward, not clean, not fresh, but different. Stale and damp and chemical, faintly metallic. It tastes of concrete and age and something sharp underneath.

I crouch at the edge and look down. A ventilation shaft, vertical, dropping into darkness. Running down its centre, fixed to the shaft wall with metal brackets, a thick black hose reinforced with steel mesh. Attached to the hose near the surface, small and expensive-looking and entirely out of place in a decommissioned system from 2018, is the pump.

Compact. Portable. Recently installed.

"This is not the school's," I say.

"Someone put it here," Aarav says. "After the building was sealed."

Which means someone has been in this building after the outbreak begins. Someone who knows about the Chemistry Lab below. Someone who wants the air from the sealed upper floors moved downward.

I do not know yet whether that someone intends this as containment or as supply. Both possibilities are disturbing in different directions.

Tanya has moved to the edge of the hole. She has a pair of rusted scissors from Mr. Dev's desk in her hand. "I will cut the power cord. We stop the pump and we stop the extraction."

"Wait," I say. "If this system has been designed by someone who knew what they were doing, cutting the power may not simply stop it. It may trigger a secondary response. A pressure release. A lock. We do not know what is on the other end of this hose."

She holds the scissors still. I look at Sid.

"Pull it out," I say. "Not the cord. The pump itself. Disconnect the suction from the hose. Use your weight."

He lowers himself to the floor. He reaches into the shaft with both hands. His hands find the pump casing. He grips it. The thrum and hiss changes pitch immediately as the suction intake is partially blocked. He pulls. At seven seconds the seal gives way with a wrenching metallic sound and the pump comes free with a hollow, pressurised crack.

Silence. The specific silence of a machine that has stopped.

Sid sits up with the pump in his hands, breathing hard, and sets it on the floor beside the hole. The air in the room does not immediately improve. That is not how physics works and I have not expected it to. But the extraction has stopped. The timeline resets to a more honest number. Better. Not good. Better.

It is Aarav who hears the tapping first.

He has been crouched near the hole since the pump comes free, his head tilted, listening to something I cannot yet hear. Then he goes completely still.

"Something heard us," he says.

And then I hear it too.

Tap. Tap-tap. Tap.

It comes from below. Not from the corridor. From below the floor, travelling up through the shaft and the concrete and the tile and into the soles of my feet in a way that is more felt than heard. Rhythmic. Deliberate. Spaced with a precision that rules out structural settling. This is a pattern. Someone has chosen these intervals.

Tap. Tap-tap. Tap.

It pauses. Then it repeats, slightly closer, as though whatever is making it has moved toward the exposed hole and is now directly below us.

Tanya backs away from the edge. "Close it. Put the tile back. Close the hole right now."

"The tile is broken," Sid says.

I do not move to close it. I am listening. There is information in that sound and information is the only thing standing between us and a series of decisions made without sufficient data.

And then, from the shaft, something else. Something that should not be possible from whatever is down there.

A cough. Wet. Rattling. Precisely replicating the sound from the classroom next door at the start of this morning. Arjun's sound.

Ishani makes a sharp involuntary noise and presses her hand over her mouth. Aarav scrambles backward from the hole. Even Tanya, who does not startle easily, goes rigid against the wall.

I do not move.

I am thinking about the people standing still in the courtyard. I am thinking about the pathogen that moves like a signal. I am thinking about the pump that someone has installed deliberately, and the system it connects to, and what kind of entity would use a dead boy's cough as a knock on a door.

The Knockers. Mr. Dev says the word quietly, the name dredging up old, half-forgotten disaster drills. I am filing it now under something different. Not a warning. A problem. And problems have solutions if you have enough variables.

"We cannot stay in this room," I say.

Nobody argues. Even Tanya does not argue.

The hole in the floor is open. The hose is still there, running down into the dark. The tapping has stopped, but it is not gone, and I understand instinctively that the pause is not a retreat. It is waiting. Everything below us is waiting to see what we do next.

I look at the hose. I look at Aarav. "You said the hose leads to the Level B Chemistry Lab."

"The filtration unit," he says. "Yes. And the air down there is cleaner than the air up here."

"Then the answer is below," I say. "We go down."

I pick up the hose and test its weight and begin, methodically, to prepare for the descent.

Hollow Floors

The descent has to be organised or it will be a disaster.

That is the first thing. Not the fear, not the tapping that has stopped but not left, not the image of Arjun's cough replicated from somewhere below the floor by something that has never met him. The first thing is the logistics, because logistics are solvable and everything else is not.

I look at the hole. I look at the people in the room. Then I start assigning.

"Sid, you go first. Your size clears the shaft and tests the walls. If something gives way under your weight it gives way now, not when someone lighter is halfway down and cannot brace for it."

He nods. He has already moved to the edge without being asked, which is either courage or the inability to tolerate waiting, and with Siddharth the difference has always been difficult to determine. Tonight it does not matter.

"Ishani, you follow him. Two body lengths above, no closer. Your job is to maintain the pace and keep it quiet. Every sound we make travels through the metal. Every sound is information we are giving away for free."

Ishani looks at the hole and then at me and nods once.

"I go third. I carry the supplies and the remaining water. Aarav, you are fourth. You know the layout below better than anyone and I need that knowledge accessible, not at the bottom waiting to be asked. Tanya, you are last."

Tanya's expression does not change. "Of course I am last."

"You are last because the person at the top controls the entry point. If something comes from the corridor while we are in the shaft, the last person is the one who seals it. That requires someone who will make the hard decision without hesitating." I hold her gaze. "You have already demonstrated you can do that."

It is not a compliment. She understands that it is not a compliment. But she also understands that it is true, and true is what she respects, and she says nothing further.

Mr. Dev has stood up from his desk. He is watching the preparation with the expression of someone who has been waiting to be assigned a role and is beginning to suspect there is not one.

"You stay," I say, before he can ask.

"The hose is anchored to your desk. If we need to come back up quickly we need something at the top that will hold our weight and yours is the heaviest piece of furniture in this room. You are the anchor. You brace the desk against the wall and you do not move it regardless of what you hear from the shaft."

A long pause. He looks at the hole. He looks at the desk. He looks at Arjun's glasses, still parallel to the edge where he has placed them.

"And if you do not come back," he says quietly.

"Then you have the supplies we are leaving and the room and however much time the air gives you." I pause. "But we will come back."

He looks at me for a moment with something I cannot fully classify, something that is not quite belief and not quite hope but is in the same neighbourhood as both.

"Right," he says. He moves to the desk and positions his feet against the baseboard. "Go, then. Before I say something encouraging and embarrass us both."

I almost smile. Almost.

The shaft is worse than I have calculated for. Going down, gravity is a passive collaborator. The cold intensifies as we descend. The shaft air is damp and carries that metallic chemical undertone, sharper now, more complex. Ethanol. Something astringent underneath. The smell of a laboratory that has been running processes for a long time in a sealed environment. Not the smell of rot. Not the smell of death. The smell of work.

Someone has been working down here.

The rule is simple and I have stated it before we descend: slow inhale, slower exhale, silent. We do not speak in the shaft.

After several metres of agonising descent, Aarav's voice comes down the shaft in a whisper barely shaped into words.

"There is a horizontal branch. Eight, maybe nine metres down from the floor. The hose goes through it. That is the junction to the lab corridor."

Sid's response comes back up. "I feel it. The hose turns. There is a door. Small. Sealed."

"Rust or lock?"

"Rust. Old rust. The seal is corrosion, not a mechanism."

Which means it can be forced. Which means Sid can force it.

"Use your shoulder," I say downward. "One controlled movement. Do not brace against the shaft wall, you will transmit the sound. Use the momentum of your own weight."

Then the sound of something giving way, not a crash, a reluctant tearing sound, metal separating from metal with the groan of something that has been fused by years of damp and disuse. Old air rushes upward past me, carrying that laboratory smell in concentrated form.

"Through," Sid says. The word comes up the shaft with the particular quality of a sound made in a larger space. He is in the corridor.

Ishani goes next. Then me. Then Aarav, who descends with the fast, boneless efficiency of someone who has apparently spent a significant portion of his school years in ventilation shafts, information I am filing under things I wish I had asked about earlier.

I reach back up for Tanya's hand as she comes through the junction. She takes it without comment. Her grip is hard and brief and she releases it the moment her feet are on the ground.

We are in.

The corridor is low-ceilinged and narrow, concrete on all sides, illuminated by a single fluorescent strip that flickers in a slow, irregular rhythm that is somehow more unsettling than darkness would have been. It casts everything in a greenish light that makes all of us look like we are already unwell.

Which, to varying degrees, we are.

The air here is better. Measurably, physically better. The heaviness at the back of the throat is less pronounced. The pressure behind my eyes has retreated slightly, enough to think more clearly, enough to feel the difference between this and what we have been breathing upstairs.

We find her thirty metres down the corridor, slumped against a cluster of thick water pipes that run floor to ceiling along the left wall.

A woman wearing a lab coat that has once been white. She looks perhaps in her late twenties, though something about the specific quality of her stillness makes age feel irrelevant. Her face is composed. That is the detail that stops me. Not the stillness, I have seen stillness today, but the specific quality of the expression: a profound, exhausted calm, the face of someone who has made a decision and is at peace with it.

She has died here deliberately. Not from violence. From the air, but slowly, quietly, with a small silver device in her hand that I recognise immediately as a portable air filtration unit, the kind used in high-contamination research environments. It is dark and silent, its battery long exhausted. She has sat here with her filtration running until it runs out, and then she has sat here without it.

On her lab coat, stitched near the collar, a small embroidered logo and below it a name.

DR. RAMEERA.

I look at the name for a long moment. I file it as the most significant variable currently in my possession and crouch down to look at what she is holding.

The silver device is not just a filtration unit. It is a key. I can see the interface port on its side, the kind designed to integrate with a specific system's access protocol. It has been built for something in this corridor.

I take it carefully from her hand. It is cold and heavier than it looks. Along the side, pressed into the metal with fine precision, small enough that I have missed it in the dimness, a line of text.

THE KEY.

She named it. Before she lost it. Leaving it for whoever comes next, with the specific, considered hope of someone who has run out of time but not out of faith in the existence of a next person.

Behind me, Ishani makes a soft sound. Not grief exactly. The sound of someone absorbing an image that will stay with them.

"Do not touch anything else," I say. "Maintain distance."

I close my hand around The Key.

And then the music starts. It comes from further down, from the direction of the lab, muffled by distance and concrete but identifiable. A piano piece. Slow, deliberate, the notes spaced with the careful precision of someone playing for an audience they want to hold in place. Debussy. Clair de Lune.

"That is not a recording," Aarav says. "The acoustics are wrong for a speaker. The resonance is coming through the pipes. They are using the pipe system as a conductor. The whole structure is acting as the instrument."

I think about the tapping from above. The rhythmic knocking through floors and walls. The cough that has replicated Arjun's death in perfect acoustic detail. Sound is their language. The pipes are their infrastructure. And right now they are playing us music.

Not to threaten. Not to warn. To guide.

"We follow it," I say.

Tanya turns to look at me. "You want to follow the sound made by the things that have been trying to get to us all night."

"They have not been trying to get to us," I say. "They have been trying to get our attention. There is a difference and it matters."

She looks at the corridor ahead. Then back at me. "If we die," she says, "I want it noted that I had reservations."

"Noted," I say.

I hold The Key and walk toward the music.

The Rules of Breathing

The music stops the moment we round the corner. Not faded. Not trailed off the way music does when a piece ends or a player loses interest. It stops with the deliberate, surgical precision of something that has achieved its purpose and no longer needs to continue.

They know we are here. They have known before the music stops. The music has never been for ambience. I keep walking.

The corridor ends at a door. Industrial steel, vault-weight, with a key card reader set into the wall beside it at eye level. A faded placard above the frame reads: LEVEL B CHEMISTRY LAB. CONTAINMENT PROTOCOLS ACTIVE.

The seams of the door are leaking air, not smoke, not gas, just the faintest displacement of atmosphere, the breath of a pressurised space exhaling slightly into a lower-pressure corridor. I hold my hand near the seam and feel it. Cooler than the corridor air. Drier. The oxygen content will be higher on the other side of this door.

I look at The Key in my hand. The interface port on its side matches the key card reader exactly.

"That opens it," Aarav says. He is at my shoulder, close enough that I can hear him thinking.

"Yes." "And whatever is in there knows we are standing outside it right now." "Yes." "Are we going in?" "Yes."

He exhales slowly. "I want it noted that I asked."

Tanya makes a sound behind us that is not quite a laugh and not quite a sob and does not develop into either.

I do not open the door immediately. I need thirty seconds and I take them, because thirty seconds of preparation is worth more than the oxygen it costs.

"The moment this door opens, do not rush the air. I know you want to. Your body is going to tell you to breathe deeply and it will be wrong. Shallow breaths, same as the shaft, until I say otherwise. Rapid oxygen intake after prolonged mild deprivation causes dizziness and in some cases temporary loss of coordination. We cannot afford either of those things in an unknown space."

Sid nods. He has been leaning against the wall with his eyes half closed in the particular way of someone managing their own physiology through sheer concentration, but he straightens now.

"Sid, you go in fast and go left. Do not stop moving until you have something solid at your back. Ishani, you follow and go right. Aarav, you stay directly behind me. Tanya, you are last through and you pull the door behind you, not closed, just pulled to."

"And if something is waiting on the other side," Tanya says.

"Then Sid's job is to put as much distance between it and the entrance as possible and our job is to move in the opposite direction while it is focused on him."

Sid looks at me. "Comforting."

"It is a role that requires the most physically capable person in the group. I am not sending you first because you are expendable. I am sending you first because you are the only one who can create enough displacement to give the rest of us options."

He looks at me for another moment. Then he moves to the door.

I insert The Key into the reader. It is accepted immediately. A soft green pulse. A mechanical sequence of sounds from within the door, tumblers releasing in order, a pneumatic hiss as the pressure differential equalises. The door begins to swing inward, heavy and slow, releasing a wave of cold dry air that hits my face like the first clean thing I have felt in eighteen hours.

Nobody breathes deeply. I am proud of all of them for that.

The lab is enormous. I have not expected it to be so enormous. Stainless steel benches run in parallel lines across the floor. Glass and tubing and equipment I can identify and equipment I cannot. At the far end of the room, taking up the entire wall, the filtration unit, a stack of interlocked metal canisters connected by dense pipework, its control interface glowing a steady green in the white light.

And in the centre of the room, standing with its back partially to us, the Knocker.

It is tall, taller than Siddharth, which I have not anticipated, and thin in a way that suggests the thinness is recent, the body consuming itself to maintain the network's processes. It is wearing a school janitor's uniform, grey and blue, with a name badge I cannot read from this distance. Its posture is entirely still, not the stillness of someone waiting passively but the stillness of something actively, continuously processing.

Sid is already moving left, fast and low. Ishani goes right. Aarav presses in behind me and I feel his hand briefly grip the back of my sleeve, not pulling, just contact, the way you reach for something solid when the ground feels uncertain.

The Knocker does not turn.

It is holding something, a small silver device identical to The Key, pressed to the side of its face. As I watch, it presses a button.

A child's cry fills the room. High and sharp and absolute, the sound of a small person in complete distress, amplified by the hard surfaces of the lab into something that seems to come from everywhere at once.

Ishani freezes. Her hands go to her ears and her face does the thing it does when she encounters suffering she cannot immediately address, a kind of full-body flinch that is not weakness but the precise opposite of it.

"It is a recording," I say. My voice comes out flat and certain even though I am not entirely certain. "Do not move toward the sound. Move toward the wall."

The crying continues. The Knocker presses the button again and the sound resets, playing from the beginning. It is testing the recording's effect on us. Measuring our responses. Collecting data on which sounds produce which movements.

I understand then that the music in the corridor has been the same thing. Not guidance. Calibration. It has been learning how we respond to sound before we arrive.

I keep my eyes on the Knocker and move toward the filtration unit.

It turns when I am twelve feet away. The face is the part that the word Knocker has not prepared me for. I have constructed something monstrous in the space where information is absent. The reality is worse than monstrous because it is human. The features are a person's features, someone's specific nose and jaw and brow, a face that has belonged to an individual before it belongs to the network. The eyes are very large and very dark, the pupils expanded beyond normal range.

It looks at me with those expanded pupils and I look back and I do not look away because looking away is information I am not willing to give it.

The child's crying stops.

In the silence that follows, the Knocker makes a sound I have not heard before. Not a tap. Not a knock. Not a replicated human sound. A soft, rapid clicking, like a camera shutter firing repeatedly, precise and rhythmic and coming from somewhere in its throat or chest.

Processing. It is processing me specifically. It has been processing all of us since we enter but it is processing me differently, with a focused attention that the others are not receiving. And I understand, standing twelve feet from it in the cold bright light of the lab, that it already knows which one of us it is waiting for.

"Aarav," I say, without taking my eyes off the Knocker. "The filtration unit. Where is the access port?"

"Left panel," he says. "There will be an interface. It will want a key."

I hold up The Key. The Knocker's clicking intensifies briefly and then slows.

I walk the remaining twelve feet to the filtration unit with the Knocker's gaze on me the entire way. I find the access port. I insert The Key.

The interface screen clears. A cursor blinks. Then text appears, line by line, in the specific unhurried rhythm of a system that has been waiting a long time to say something.

PROTOCOL ACCESSED. FILTRATION UNIT: STABLE. CONTAMINANT DISPERSAL: SUSPENDED. HOST EVALUATION: IN PROGRESS.

I stare at the last line. Behind me, the Knocker's clicking has stopped entirely. The lab is absolutely silent except for the hum of the filtration unit and the sound of five people breathing, carefully, the way I have taught them.

The Knocker moves. Not toward me. Away, one fluid step backward into the shadows at the edge of the lab, clearing the centre of the room. It is not defeated. It is attentive. Watching. The screen blinks steadily.

The system is counting something. The counting is not finished.

Under the Fluorescents

Nobody moves for a long moment after I say it.

The lab found us.

The words sit in the cold bright air of the room and do what true things do when they are said out loud for the first time, they rearrange everything that has come before them into a new configuration that cannot be undone. We have not been surviving. We have been sorted.

Tanya is the first to speak. "Sorted for what."

"I do not know yet."

"That is not an acceptable answer."

"It is the only one I have." I look at the screen. "What I know is that the system has been evaluating us since before we came down here. Possibly since before the outbreak. The pump is not sabotaged. It is a pressure gradient, moving the least contaminated air downward and leaving us with a choice: suffocate in the room or follow the air. We follow the air. That is the first correct decision as far as the system is concerned."

"We had no other option," Sid says.

"The system does not distinguish between a choice made freely and a choice made under constraint. It measures the outcome and whether the outcome is oriented toward collective survival. We come down together. We do not leave anyone behind." I pause. "Anyone who is still alive."

The room absorbs this.

Aarav has drifted toward the filtration unit, his notebook open, his eyes moving between the screen and the pipework with the focused hunger of someone who has been waiting his entire academic life for a problem this interesting and is furious at himself for finding it interesting under these circumstances.

Ishani is looking at my hand. At The Key.

"There is something on it," she says. I look down. She crosses to me and holds out her hand, not asking, just offering the light from a different angle, and I turn the object so that it catches the full brightness of the overhead fluorescents. Along the side, pressed into the metal, a line of text.

THE KEY.

"She named it," Ishani says softly. "Before she lost it."

I think about Dr. Rameera sitting in the corridor with her filtration unit running down, knowing what she is leaving behind. Naming the thing she cannot take with her. Leaving it for whoever comes next with the specific, considered hope of someone who has run out of time but not out of faith in the existence of a next person. She knew someone would come. She built the system to find them. I close my hand around The Key. From that moment it has a name and the name is not a label. It is an inheritance.

The Knocker has not moved from the shadows at the edge of the lab. It has stepped back when the screen displays its message. It has not attacked. It has not advanced. It has not replicated any sound since the child's cry cuts off. It has been doing exactly this for eleven minutes, which is long enough that the initial terror of its presence has begun, for some of us, to shift into something more complicated. Not comfort. Not trust. Something more like the uneasy truce you reach with a large animal when you have both concluded that aggression is not currently in either party's interest.

"The screen," Aarav says. He is still at the filtration unit. "There is a sub-directory here. Behind the main display. After The Key gets past the first layer it opens into a larger architecture."

"What kind of architecture," I say.

"A log. Research notes. Someone has been documenting everything that happened in this lab, going back at least two years." He looks up at me. "Going back to before the outbreak."

Two years. The outbreak happens today. The system has been running for at least two years before the day the school seals and the loudspeaker says one word and dies.

Whatever this is, it has not started this morning. This morning has simply been the day it becomes impossible to ignore.

I cross to the unit and look at the screen over Aarav's shoulder. The sub-directory has opened into a list of dated entries, the most recent timestamped at 11:16 a.m. today, forty-seven minutes after the coughing starts in the classroom next door. I scroll to the most recent entry.

The text is in the compressed, precise style of someone accustomed to recording observations under time pressure, every word chosen for maximum information density and minimum ambiguity.

11:16, Dr. Rameera, Final Entry "System stable. Personal status: failing. Contamination event accelerated beyond projected timeline. Containment of upper floors unsuccessful. Expect breach within six to eight hours. If this reaches a viable host: the network is not the threat. The network is the response. What it responds to is the question you need to answer. I built The Key for the person who could ask it."

The entry ends there. No signature. No timestamp for whatever comes after, because what comes after is Dr. Rameera sitting in a corridor with a filtration unit counting down its last reserves.

I straighten up. The room is very quiet. Aarav has stopped writing. Even Tanya has gone still near the door.

"The network is not the threat," Sid says slowly. "Then what is?"

"Whatever the network is responding to," I say. "Which means we need to understand what exists before the network. What creates the conditions that the network develops in response to."

"The Chemistry Lab," Aarav says. "Level B. This is Level B. We are standing in it."

"Then the answer is here," I say. "Somewhere in this room or in those logs or in the architecture of this system. Dr. Rameera spent two years building something that could find a person capable of asking the right question. We owe it to her to ask it."

Ishani is looking at the Knocker in the shadows. Her expression has changed in the way it changes when she is processing something emotionally complex and refusing to simplify it for the sake of comfort.

"He is someone's person," she says quietly. "The Knocker. He was someone before."

Nobody responds immediately, because it is true and true things sometimes need a moment of space before anyone can speak around them.

The Knocker's clicking resumes, very soft, very slow. Not the rapid processing rhythm from earlier. Something more like the sound a person makes when they are thinking. Not at full speed. Just present. Just here.

Tanya breaks the silence, because Tanya always breaks silences that are becoming too human for her to tolerate. "So," she says. "What is the right question?"

I look at The Key in my hand. I look at the screen with Dr. Rameera's last words on it. I look at the Knocker, which is watching me with its expanded dark eyes from the shadows, waiting with the patient, specific attention of something that has been waiting for exactly this moment for a very long time.

"Not what the network is," I say. "What is the network trying to protect?"

The clicking stops. In the silence that follows, the filtration unit hums its steady note, and the fluorescents burn their clean white light, and somewhere above us, in a classroom that still smells of floor polish and chalk dust, a teacher sits alone with a dead student's glasses and waits to find out if anyone is coming back.

The answer is yes. But first we have to understand what we have walked into.

I insert The Key back into the interface and begin to read.

The Listening Room

The logs go back two years, three months, and eleven days.

I do not read all of them that night. There are too many and the air, though better than upstairs, is still not clean enough to sustain the kind of sustained focus that two years of research notes demand. I read selectively, the way you triage a problem when you have limited resources, identifying the entries most likely to contain the highest density of critical information and reading those first while Aarav reads the others and summarises in the compressed shorthand he uses when his brain is moving faster than his mouth.

What we learn in the first hour:

The fungal network has not been engineered. It has been discovered, in a soil sample taken from a construction site three kilometres from the school, during routine environmental testing eighteen months before the outbreak. The sample has been flagged as anomalous because the mycelial structure it contains is unlike any catalogued species, its signal transmission architecture is orders of magnitude more complex than anything previously observed, and it responds to human neural tissue in ways that no known fungal organism did or should.

Dr. Rameera has been the junior researcher assigned to the sample. She has been twenty-three years old.

She spent the following eighteen months attempting to understand what she had found. Her early entries are methodical and excited, the notes of someone who understands she is looking at something significant and is determined to document it correctly. Her later entries are methodical and terrified, the notes of someone who understands she is looking at something that has already understood her back.

The network is not aggressive. This is the finding that has taken Dr. Rameera the longest to accept because it contradicts every instinct she developed in response to watching it work. It does not spread to kill. It spreads to connect. The distinction is critical and also, she writes in an entry dated four months before the outbreak, almost impossible to communicate to anyone who has not spent eighteen months watching it make decisions.

Because it does make decisions. The network evaluates its environment and it makes choices based on those evaluations and the choices are consistently oriented toward one outcome: the maintenance of conditions suitable for continued complex life. It is, Dr. Rameera has written, the most aggressively alive thing she has ever encountered. Not aggressive toward life. Aggressive on behalf of it.

I stop reading at this entry and look up at the Knocker in the shadows. It is watching me. It is always watching me. But there is something in the quality of its attention now that I have not been able to classify before and am beginning to be able to classify now: it is waiting for me to understand something specific, with the patience of something that has all the time available and is simply waiting for the variable to resolve.

I look back at the screen.

The outbreak has not been an accident. This is the entry from six weeks before today, and it is the one that changes the shape of everything. Dr. Rameera has written it in a different register from her other entries, faster, less precise, the handwriting of someone typing with her whole body rather than just her hands.

Dr. Rameera, 6 weeks prior "The network does not spread randomly. I have been wrong about this for eighteen months and the wrongness has cost time I did not have to spend. It spreads in response to threats. Specifically, in response to environmental conditions that the network's processing architecture identifies as incompatible with the sustained complex life it is oriented toward maintaining. The air quality data from the past fourteen months is unambiguous. The contamination levels in the soil and groundwater within a three kilometre radius of the collection site have increased by a factor that the network would classify, if it classified things the way we do, as catastrophic. The construction. The chemical runoff. The specific combination of industrial byproducts accumulating in the substrate. The network is not the cause of the outbreak. The network is the outbreak's response to the cause. It is attempting, in the only way available to it, to create conditions under which the contamination can be contained and the environment restored. The Knockers are not infected people. They are people the network has recruited, in the only way it knows how, to serve as nodes in a remediation system. Their function is not destruction. Their function is maintenance."

I sit with it for a moment. Outside the system I am aware of the lab around me, the hum of the filtration unit, the sound of Aarav's pen moving across his notebook, Sid's careful breathing from the left wall, Ishani sitting cross-legged on the floor with her eyes closed in the specific way she has of processing information that is too large to hold upright.

Tanya has said nothing for forty minutes. This is, in my experience of Tanya, either very good or very bad, and I do not currently have the bandwidth to determine which.

I look at the Knocker. It is still watching me. But its posture has changed, a slight forward orientation, the angle of attention shifting from waiting to something more like recognition. It knows I have read the entry. It has been waiting for me to read the entry.

"Aarav," I say. "In the early entries. The soil sample. Did she document the specific conditions under which the network initiates Host selection?"

The sound of pages turning rapidly. "Entry eleven," he says. "The network does not select for intelligence or for strength. It selects for coherence. Her word, coherence. The alignment between a subject's stated reasoning and their actual decision patterns." A pause. "She means people who do what they believe and believe what they do. People who are not lying to themselves."

I think about this. I think about every time I have told myself I was calculating when I was actually caring. Every time I have described a moral choice as a logical one because logic feels more reliable. Every time I have looked at someone I am responsible for and felt something I immediately converted into a number because the number is easier to hold.

Fourteen decisions. The system has been counting something I have not been counting.

"The network is not lying to itself either," Ishani says. Her eyes are still closed. "That is why it recognised you. Like recognises like."

The lab is very quiet.

Then from the shadows, the Knocker makes a sound. Not the clicking. Not a replicated human sound. Something new, a low resonant tone that seems to come not just from its throat but from its chest and the air around it, as though the sound is being produced by something larger than a single body. It lasts approximately four seconds and then it stops.

"What was that," Tanya says from the door. Her voice has lost its flatness. There is something in it I have not heard from Tanya before, something that is not quite fear and not quite wonder but is in the territory between them.

"I think," Aarav says very carefully, "that was the network saying yes."

We do not sleep in the lab. What we do instead is organise, because organisation is the thing I can offer and the thing the group needs.

The filtration unit is stable and running. The air in the lab is the cleanest available in the building. We have water from the supply run, the food from the staff room, and the biscuits Arjun has carried. We have Aarav's structural knowledge of the building and his extraordinary capacity for absorbing technical information. We have Sid's physical capability. We have Ishani's ability to hold the group together at the level where logic cannot reach. And we have a Knocker standing in the shadows that is, according to everything Dr. Rameera has spent two years documenting, not our enemy.

I look at it. It looks at me.

"I need to go back up," I say. "Mr. Dev has been alone for hours. He has the supplies we left and the room but he does not have information, and information is the difference between fear and function. I need to bring him down here."

Sid pushes off from the wall. "I will come." His arm is bruised from the Knocker's grip, the tendons stiffened. "It works," he says. "It hurts, but it works."

I look at him for a moment. The shame from the night before is still there, I can see it, but it has been joined by something else now, the quiet, determined orientation of a person who has identified what they are for and is moving toward it.

"You go first in the shaft," I say. "Same as before."

He nods once. I look at Ishani. "You have the lab. You have Aarav. If Tanya does anything that compromises the filtration unit you have my permission to be as direct with her as the situation requires."

Ishani opens her eyes fully and looks at me with an expression that is partly amusement and partly something more serious. "Go," she says. "Bring him back."

I pick up The Key from the interface where I have left it and put it in my pocket. I go.

The Filtration Unit

The shaft is worse going up. Going down, gravity has been a passive collaborator. Going up it is simply an opponent, constant and indifferent, and the muscles that have been burning since the descent now have to reverse the direction of that burn with the same resources they have been spending for the past several hours.

We do not speak in the shaft. This is the rule and we both hold it.

The classroom is exactly as we have left it. This should not have surprised me and does anyway, the particular shock of a space that has been static while you have changed beyond recognition. The chairs are still pushed against the walls. The supply bag is still in the corner. Arjun's glasses are still parallel to the desk edge.

Mr. Dev is sitting on the floor beside his desk with his back against it and his knees drawn up and his eyes open. He looks at us when we come through the hole and something in his face shifts in a way that is too complex to read, too many things happening at once across features that have been holding themselves still for a long time.

He says, "You're late."

His voice is rough and quiet and the joke lands differently than his jokes usually land. Not as deflection. As relief made briefly audible before being put away again.

"We found things," I say. "Things that change the shape of what is happening. You need to know them."

"I assumed," he says, "given that you came back."

I sit down on the floor and I tell Mr. Dev everything.

He listens the way he always listens to things that matter, with complete stillness and complete attention, the sarcasm set aside entirely, the wit that he uses as weather-proofing stripped back to reveal the intelligence underneath, which is considerable and which he spends most of his professional life disguising as entertainment.

I tell him about the corridor and Dr. Rameera's body and The Key with its engraved name. I tell him about the lab and the Knocker and the child's cry that has been calibration rather than threat. I tell him about the filtration unit and the system log and the fourteen decision points and what the system has determined about the Host. I tell him about the logs, the soil sample, the network's origin and its orientation, the contamination that has triggered the spread, the remediation function that the Knockers are serving. I tell him that the network is not the threat. That it has been trying, in the only language available to it, to find someone who could help it do what it is built to do.

He is quiet for a long time after I finish. Then he says, "Dr. Rameera." "Yes." "Junior researcher. Twenty-three years old." "According to the early log entries, yes."

He is quiet again. He picks up Arjun's glasses from the desk and turns them over in his hands, the same slow rotation, the same careful handling, as though the glasses are a question he is trying to find the answer to by touch.

I do not say what I have noticed in the logs and have not said aloud yet, because I am not certain and because certainty is the minimum requirement before handing that particular piece of information to Siddharth. What I have noticed is a line in one of the earlier entries, a personal note Dr. Rameera has included almost as an aside, a single line that has nothing to do with the research and everything to do with the person conducting it.

She has written: Haven't been home in eight months. Sid will be furious.

It is possible it means nothing. Sid is not an uncommon name. But I have logged it, the way I log everything, in the part of my mind where unresolved variables wait for more data. I will tell him when I am certain. Not before.

"We need to bring you down," I say to Mr. Dev. "The air is significantly better in the lab. And the information in those logs is going to require more than one mind to fully understand."

Density Map

Aarav works through the night.

The walkie-talkies are placed at intervals throughout the accessible hotel floors, one per level from three to eight, with one additional unit positioned in the glass walkway junction to measure the differential between the mall-side architecture and the hotel-side. The signal interference patterns Aarav is measuring are subtle and require multiple calibration passes. He does six passes through the accessible range before he considers the data stable enough to map.

He works the way he always works when he has found a problem large enough and specific enough to fully occupy the part of his brain that generates fear when left idle. Methodical and fast, his handwriting in the notebook becoming smaller and more compressed as the night goes on and the data becomes more complex. The tiny encrypted script that I have been watching him produce for three years, which covers page after page in lines so dense they look like texture rather than language.

He is, I recognise, exactly as frightened as he always is. He is also exactly as functional as he always is when the problem is large enough. These two things coexist in him without resolving. They have always coexisted. The fear does not go away. It just stops being the loudest thing in the room.

The map, when it is finished, confirms the distribution Dr. Rameera's logs describe with one significant additional detail. The network is not evenly distributed through the hotel tower. It is denser in the sections of the tower that share infrastructure with the mall below, specifically the western stairwell which runs adjacent to the mall's core utility shaft, and the floor three corridor which connects directly to the mall's third level through the service passage. The eastern stairwell, which is further from the mall's infrastructure and draws on a separate utility loop, shows measurably lower density.

This matters. It means our current stairwell access is the safer option. It means the suite's position on the eastern side of floor six is not accidental, the system has been pointing us here since the lab map appeared on the screen.

Thickest at basement and ground. Thinning above four. Negligible above eight. We are on six, in the eastern section, with the walkway barricaded. The buffer is real. It is not permanent.

Tanya watches him work. She has been sitting on the floor near the window since the others went to sleep, Sonia against her chest. She has become, I have noticed, very good at the logistics of this, the feeding schedule, the temperature regulation, the particular angle of holding that produces the least distress. She has become good at it without making it look like she is becoming good at it, the way she becomes good at everything: quietly, without announcement, as though the competence has simply appeared rather than been built.

She watches Aarav arrange the walkie-talkies and adjust his methodology and frown at the results with the expression of someone who has found something specific to pay attention to and is paying it full attention.

"What is the frequency range you are looking at?" she asks.

Aarav looks up from his notebook. The question has landed in the space between them with a different quality from the questions Tanya usually produces, which are operational, resource-focused, framed toward whatever gives her the most information relevant to the specific calculation she is running. This is not that. This is genuine curiosity and the shape of it, arriving from Tanya, is unusual enough that Aarav takes a moment before he answers. He tells her. She asks a follow-up question about the interference pattern at the threshold levels. He answers that too. She nods once and goes back to watching Sonia sleep.

Two lines. I note it and I do not say anything about it, because Tanya's movement toward things is always better treated as assumed than acknowledged, and because what is important about those two lines is not the content but what they are not, which is a manipulation, which is a calculation, which is anything other than a person asking a question because they want to know the answer.

The rest of the suite settles into the particular quality of the small hours after sustained crisis. Aarav's mother asleep on the sofa with her phone in her hand, turned dark now, battery dead since the afternoon, still held because the holding is the thing and not the function. His father in the armchair across from her, awake, watching her sleep with the expression of a man who has known this woman for a long time and finds the watching itself sustaining.

What the Rat Knows

Jung has not spoken since the food court. He watches everything.

He has a way of distributing his attention across a room that is not the passive watching of someone who has withdrawn from participation. It is active, it is systematic, it has the quality of a process rather than a mood. He moves his eyes from person to person with the regular interval of someone who has decided on a methodology and is executing it, the same way Aarav monitors the walkie-talkie positions, the same way I run the inventory numbers, the same way my mom cycles through the suite's resources. He is doing the same thing we are all doing. He has simply chosen people as his data.

He watches Aarav work on the density map and at one point, without being asked and without drawing attention to the fact that he is doing it, he holds two walkie-talkies in position at opposite ends of the room for twenty minutes while Aarav takes readings, understanding without being told that the calibration requires a fixed reference point and a mobile comparison point and that he can be the fixed reference point without this being difficult or remarkable.

He watches Tanya feed Sonia and at some point positions himself near the kitchenette and begins handing her things she needs before she asks for them, the small bottle of formula, a cloth from the pile his mother has found, doing this with the specific efficiency of someone who has been paying attention long enough that the needs of the task have become legible.

He watches his mother and the specific way she holds Sonia, the difference between the holding when Sonia is awake and the holding when she is asleep, the way Grace's hands move differently depending on which mode they are in. And I watch him watch this and understand that he is filing the same thing I am filing: his mother is managing. She is managing through the same mechanism she always uses, which is Sonia, which is the thing that requires her attention and her presence and her function every three to four hours without fail.

He watches me. Not all the time. Not in a way that would read as surveillance. But regularly, in the rotation, with the quality of someone who has identified me as the person in this room whose next move will determine the group's next move and who wants to have the data ready when that happens. I let him watch. I do not perform anything for the watching.

Nox comes through the ventilation panel on the second day, through the gap in the maintenance access near the skirting board in the corner of the suite's sitting room. The rat moves out into the room without hurry, explores the perimeter along the skirting, pauses at intervals with its pink nose lifted, reading the air with the thorough attention it brings to every new space. Its white fur is exactly as clean and improbably bright as it always is, the specific brightness I first noted in the vent above the chalkboard on Thursday morning and filed under impossible to explain.

I understand now what it means, that brightness, that unconcerned health. The network routes around rather than through a neural architecture it cannot colonise. Nox has been living in the same infrastructure the network lives in, moving through the same shafts and ducts, breathing the same air, and the network has simply moved around it the whole time. Left it intact. Let it be. Two things sharing the same space without conflict because the conflict requires a specific kind of interface and the interface is not there.

If a rat can coexist with the network, the scale of what is possible is larger than I have been calculating for. I note this and file it. The time for saying it aloud has not yet arrived.

Nox finishes its perimeter survey and sits in the middle of the sitting room floor and looks at the room. Jung is on the eastern wall, his back against it, his knees up, watching. He looks at Nox for a moment with the quality of attention he brings to everything, unhurried and complete.

Then he holds out his hand. He holds it out at the level of the floor, palm up, the way you hold a hand out to something small, and he is still, very still, the total stillness of someone who has understood that this kind of contact requires being not-moving, being not-threatening, being simply available.

Nox looks at the hand. Nox crosses the floor.

Across the suite, Sid watches this happen. He is sitting against the bedroom door with his eyes half open, the managed stillness he has had since we arrived at the hotel, not sleeping, present in a specific way that is about containment rather than rest. He watches Jung's hand and Nox coming toward it and something moves across his face that is not something I have seen from Siddharth before. Something that is not grief and is not relief and is not the expression he has for ordinary things. Something that resembles the expression he has when he is watching me make a decision he already knows is the right one, the expression of recognition, the sense of something clicking into the place it belongs.

Nox reaches Jung's hand and touches it with its nose and stays.

The rat has chosen proximity. Not shelter, not warmth, not the resource-motivated closeness of an animal that has calculated that being near a person is advantageous. Something else. The most biologically intact living thing in this building, the thing the network cannot touch and cannot colonise and cannot use, has chosen to be beside this specific boy who has not spoken since his father locked a washroom door from the inside.

I file all of it. The coexistence. The choosing. The specific quality of what passes over Sid's face when he watches it.

I do not say any of it aloud yet. The time will come. There are things that need to be in place before the saying is useful. There are things I am still carrying, the variable in Dr. Rameera's log, the eight months away, the specific name. Things I will put down when the moment is right and not before.

Jung does not speak. But something in his face has shifted in a way that is small and belongs entirely to him. Nox settles beside him and the afternoon light comes through the hotel window and falls across both of them, the boy and the rat, sitting quietly in the middle of everything, and the room continues around them, and it is enough.
